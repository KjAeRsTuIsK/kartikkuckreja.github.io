The question behind the work

Can we trust what machines say they see?

Modern multimodal models describe images fluently — but fluency isn't faithfulness. My research attacks this gap from three directions: building models whose answers are grounded in pixels, building forensic tools for media where the pixels themselves lie, and building benchmarks that catch models pretending.

AREA 01

Synthetic media & deepfake forensics

Detection accuracy is no longer enough — a detector that can't explain why a video is fake won't be trusted in a newsroom or a courtroom. I work on interpretable and explainable foundation models for synthetic media detection: judges that verify whether a detector's reasoning is actually grounded in visual artifacts (DeepfakeJudge, CVPR 2026; XPlainVerse), and datasets that stress detectors where they're weakest — multilingual, code-switched speech (ArEnAV, ICLR 2026), real-world perturbations at million-video scale (AV-Deepfake1M++), and under-represented populations (INDIFACE, FG 2024).

AREA 02

Grounded vision–language models

A model that can point at what it's talking about is harder to fool and easier to trust. GeoChat (CVPR 2024) — my undergraduate thesis with Prof. Fahad Khan, Dr. Salman Khan and Dr. Abhijit Das — was the first grounded large VLM for remote sensing: it answers questions about satellite imagery while localizing its evidence with boxes. More recently I've explored how grounding emerges in the first place, with object-first inductive biases for language learning from child-view video (Objects Before Words).

AREA 03

Benchmarks that keep models honest

Progress follows measurement. I've helped build evaluations that models can't shortcut: ALM-Bench (CVPR 2025 Highlight) tests cultural and linguistic coverage across 100 languages; GEOBench-VLM (ICCV 2025 Highlight) probes geospatial reasoning with 10,000+ manually verified tasks. Earlier work includes long movie understanding and deepfake benchmarking.
