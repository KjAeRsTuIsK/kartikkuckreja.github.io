Research

Multimodal models are now used to generate, describe, and verify media. This creates two related risks: media that is fabricated, and model reasoning that cannot be checked. My work addresses both. Most of it falls into the areas below.

Synthetic media detection

Deepfake detection performance depends heavily on what the training and evaluation data cover, and existing datasets leave large gaps. I have worked on closing several of them: ArEnAV (ICLR 2026) is the first audio-visual deepfake dataset with Arabic-English code-switched and dialectal speech, where current detectors degrade sharply; AV-Deepfake1M++ scales to over a million videos with the perturbations typical of media shared online, and underpins the 1M-Deepfakes Detection Challenge; INDIFACE (FG 2024) covers faces from the Indian subcontinent, which mainstream datasets underrepresent.

Interpretability and explanation faithfulness

Detectors increasingly justify their predictions in natural language, but a fluent explanation is not necessarily a faithful one: models often cite artifacts that are not present in the input. This gap matters anywhere a human has to act on the output. DeepfakeJudge (CVPR 2026) uses a bootstrapped MLLM-as-a-judge to score whether a detector's reasoning is grounded in visual evidence, and uses those scores as supervision to improve reasoning quality. XPlainVerse extends this direction with a million-scale benchmark for evaluating explanation faithfulness in deepfake detection. This line of work is my main current focus.

Evaluation of multimodal models

Claims about model capability are only as good as the evaluations behind them. ALM-Bench (CVPR 2025, Highlight) measures cultural and linguistic coverage of large multimodal models across 100 languages, including many low-resource ones. GEOBench-VLM (ICCV 2025, Highlight) evaluates vision-language models on geospatial tasks — counting, localization, change detection — with over 10,000 manually verified instructions.

Grounded vision-language models

A model that localizes the evidence for its answers is easier to audit than one that only produces text. My undergraduate thesis, GeoChat (CVPR 2024), built the first grounded large vision-language model for remote sensing: it answers questions about satellite imagery while returning the regions its answers refer to. More recently, Objects Before Words studies object-first inductive biases for grounding language in egocentric child-view video.
